The core addition to the book

The existing core claim is:

Knowledge now melts faster than organizations can re-bind it into identity, norms, and operating systems without depleting trust.

The new addition is:

Agency now scales faster than organizations can preserve low-loss intent across distributed human and machine action.

Those are not the same claim.

The first is about knowledge validity.
The second is about execution coherence.

So the revised master frame becomes:

AGI creates two pressures at once. It accelerates epistemic melt, because knowledge becomes easier to create, contest, and destroy. It also multiplies agency, because more actors and agents can now turn current knowledge into action. Ritual Time handles the first pressure by updating knowledge and repairing the organization after knowledge-change. Run Time handles the second pressure by maximizing coherent agency under current doctrine.

This is the clean distinction to protect throughout the revisions.

New chapter 1: The Dynamo and the Agent

Purpose

This chapter should become the historical hook. It gives readers a concrete, high-Lindy analogy before the abstract theory arrives.

The central lesson:

When a general-purpose technology removes an old bottleneck, the first instinct is to install it inside the old organization. The real gains come only after the organization is redesigned around the new bottleneck.

The electrification story supports this well. The shift from water/steam prime movers with shafts and belts to electric motors that could drive individual machines unfolded between roughly 1880 and 1930. The early change reduced energy costs, but the deeper gain came when electricity allowed factories to redesign layout, material handling, downtime patterns, and throughput.  ￼

David and Wright describe a “productivity pause” of roughly three decades before U.S. manufacturing productivity surged in the 1920s. Their account emphasizes that electricity’s gains required not just a new energy source but complementary managerial, organizational, and conceptual changes.  ￼

Suggested chapter outline

1. The line shaft world

Explain the pre-electric factory as a physical image.

A central prime mover powered shafts, belts, pulleys, and machines. Because physical power had to be distributed mechanically, the factory’s layout was organized around the power system. Machines were arranged according to access to power, not necessarily according to the best flow of materials.

Key point:

When physical power was scarce and centralized, the factory organized around power distribution.

2. The first mistake: electrifying the old factory

Early electrification often preserved the old logic. Instead of redesigning the factory, firms replaced or supplemented the prime mover with electric power while continuing to use group-drive systems. The new technology was installed into the old architecture.

Key phrase:

They bolted the dynamo to the old line shaft.

This is the analogy to firms that use AGI inside existing job descriptions, approval chains, org charts, and meeting structures.

3. The real breakthrough: unit drive

The real breakthrough came when electric motors were attached to individual machines. This allowed machines to be placed according to process flow rather than power transmission. David and Wright note that unit drive made possible single-story, linear layouts, faster and more reliable material flow, reduced backtracking, lighter buildings, more modular downtime, and greater capital utilization.  ￼

Key point:

The new power source mattered less than the new layout it made possible.

4. The bottleneck moved

The old bottleneck was physical power. Once electricity relieved that bottleneck, the new constraints became material flow, information flow, scheduling, layout, coordination, and management.

This is the historical bridge:

Electrification did not merely make factories stronger. It changed what factories were organized around.

5. The post-AGI parallel

Pre-AGI organizations are largely organized around competent labor capacity for knowledge work. People mediate work because people historically held scarce judgment, skill, memory, context, and execution capacity.

AGI changes that.

The new bottleneck is not “Can we do enough knowledge work?” It becomes:

Can we preserve low-loss intent as agency multiplies?

6. The chapter’s closing law

End the chapter with the new law:

An organization scales until its agency exceeds its coherence. Beyond that point, agency becomes an accelerant of incoherence.

Then hand off to the next chapter.

New chapter 2: The Coherence Ceiling

Purpose

This is the theoretical chapter readers can be pointed to for the new model.

Core definition:

Coherence is low-loss intent across distributed agency.

Core claim:

Once competent execution capacity becomes abundant, coherence becomes the dominant scaling constraint.

Suggested chapter outline

1. Define agency

Agency should be defined carefully.

Agency is the capacity to perceive, choose, act, and create commitments in the world.

This includes humans, teams, software agents, AI workflows, automated sales systems, code-generating agents, support agents, and decision-support loops.

Important distinction:

* Latent agency: capacity available but not deployed.
* Bounded agency: capacity operating inside doctrine, permissions, evals, and feedback.
* Unbounded agency: capacity acting faster than the organization can integrate, correct, or metabolize.

Key line:

Agency is not output. Agency is the power to make things happen.

2. Define coherence

Use the phrase you liked:

Coherence is low-loss intent across distributed agency.

Then unpack it:

Coherence means the organization can preserve intent as vision becomes strategy, strategy becomes doctrine, doctrine becomes decisions, decisions become actions, actions create feedback, and feedback updates doctrine.

It is not agreement. It is not conformity. It is not vibe. It is not “everyone saying the same words.”

Better definition:

Coherence is regulated variety in service of shared essential variables.

3. The coherence ceiling

Define the core construct.

The Coherence Ceiling is the point at which added agency stops increasing useful throughput because the organization can no longer preserve shared intent, context, trust, and correction across the actions being generated.

Then define the sign flip:

Agency inversion is the point beyond the coherence ceiling where marginal agency becomes harmful.

Key lines to preserve:

Below the coherence ceiling, agency becomes throughput. Above it, agency becomes contradiction.

Past the coherence ceiling, every new agent is a faster way to be wrong.

The post-AGI danger is not insufficient intelligence. It is more intelligence than the organization can metabolize.

4. Simon: bounded rationality becomes bounded coherence

Use Simon to show why AGI does not simply solve the organization.

Simon’s theory of bounded rationality rejected the idea of omniscient optimizing decision-makers; his work emphasized limits in alternatives, information, consequences, attention, and computation. His Nobel lecture describes bounded rationality as an alternative to classical omniscient rationality, with mechanisms such as search, satisficing, authority, and employment relations.  ￼

Post-AGI translation:

AGI expands local rationality, but it does not abolish organizational bounded rationality.

The organization still has to decide which premises are valid, which tradeoffs dominate, which customer promises are canonical, which doctrine is current, and which action counts as “us.”

Key construct:

Doctrine is how an organization bounds rationality without merely reducing intelligence.

This should become one of the chapter’s central ideas.

5. Galbraith: information-processing demand versus capacity

Galbraith’s information-processing view says task uncertainty shapes organization design. He describes responses such as creating self-contained tasks, investing in vertical information systems, and creating lateral networks.  ￼

Post-AGI translation:

AGI increases information-processing capacity, but it can also explode information-processing demand.

Every agent can now create plans, variants, documents, analyses, objections, code, forecasts, workflows, and customer interactions. The firm may not be starved for information. It may drown in it.

Key line:

When agency exceeds coherence, the organization does not get smarter. It gets louder.

Galbraith gives you the design question:

Should we increase processing capacity, reduce processing demand, or restructure the work so less coordination is necessary?

This supports your direct doctrine-to-agent idea.

6. Thompson: uncertainty, the technical core, and interdependence

Thompson’s Organizations in Action treats organizations as complex systems facing uncertainty; his central thesis includes how organizations handle uncertainty through technologies, environments, and organizational structure.  ￼

Use Thompson to distinguish where direct doctrine-to-agent execution works best.

* Pooled interdependence: units contribute separately to a whole. Standardization works.
* Sequential interdependence: one output becomes another input. Planning and interfaces matter.
* Reciprocal interdependence: parts continuously affect one another. Mutual adjustment is necessary.

Post-AGI translation:

Route modular work through doctrine-guided agents. Route reciprocal, ambiguous, high-stakes work through Actor clusters with agent support.

Key line:

Do not mediate all work through people. Mediate uncertainty through the right boundary.

7. Ashby: coherence as regulation, not agreement

Ashby’s law of requisite variety says a regulator must have enough variety to absorb the disturbances it faces; in active regulation, “only variety can destroy variety.” ScienceDirect’s summary also emphasizes that increasing the variety of action must be accompanied by knowing which action to select.  ￼

Post-AGI translation:

AGI increases agency-variety. Therefore the organization must increase coherence-variety.

But Ashby also prevents a bad interpretation:

The answer is not less variety. The answer is regulated variety.

This protects the book from treating coherence as conformity.

Key phrase:

Coherence without conformity.

Another:

Shared premises, divergent probes.

8. Run Time versus Ritual Time, sharpened

This is the main integration with The Dionysus Program.

Current definition: Run Time is ordinary operations; Ritual Time is where performance scoring pauses and the group metabolizes loss under the rules of the rite.  ￼

New definition:

Run Time is where current doctrine is converted into coherent agency.

Ritual Time is where doctrine is updated after reality falsifies it, and where the organization repairs the social substrate damaged by knowledge-change.

This addition is meaningful because it gives Run Time its own active discipline.

Current Run Time says: execute, decide, be accountable.
New Run Time says: maximize coherent agency under current doctrine.

Suggested 2x2 to include:

	High coherence	Low coherence
Valid knowledge	Productive Run Time	Agentic decoherence
Invalid knowledge	Coherent wrongness	Collapse / churn / anomie

This may be one of the most useful additions in the whole revision.

9. Theory of Constraints applied to coherence

Use TOC as the operating method. TOC’s five focusing steps are identify the constraint, exploit it, subordinate everything else to it, elevate it, and repeat/prevent inertia.  ￼

Post-AGI version:

1. Identify the coherence constraint
    Where does intent degrade?
2. Exploit coherence
    Stop wasting coherence on unnecessary initiatives, stale decisions, ambiguous handoffs, and unbounded agentic work.
3. Subordinate agency to coherence
    Do not deploy more autonomous agency than the current doctrine, evals, interfaces, and feedback loops can integrate.
4. Elevate coherence
    Build doctrine systems, context stewards, agent evals, Actor feedback loops, Ritual Time cadence, and decision memory.
5. Repeat
    Once coherence is no longer the constraint, the bottleneck may move to demand, regulation, capital, compute, physical operations, or trust outside the firm.

Key TOC line:

Do not add agency to a coherence-constrained system. First exploit and elevate coherence.

New chapter 3: Auteurs, Agents, Actors

Purpose

This is the organizational design chapter. It turns the theory into a memorable model.

Core frame:

Auteurs author doctrine. Agents execute doctrine. Actors test doctrine against reality.

Suggested chapter outline

1. Why the old org chart decays

The old organization was built around scarce competent labor capacity. Managers allocated, supervised, coordinated, reviewed, approved, and corrected labor.

Post-AGI, that default structure often becomes the new line shaft. It routes work through people even when people are no longer the best middleware.

Key line:

People should not be the middleware. Doctrine should be the middleware.

But add the caveat:

Minimize humans as relay nodes; preserve humans as authors, sensors, arbiters, and accountable interfaces.

2. Auteurs

Auteurs are responsible for vision, taste, identity, myth, doctrine, and essential variables.

They answer:

What is this organization?
What is it trying to become?
What must not be violated?
What counts as excellence?
What counts as betrayal?
What local success would be globally wrong?

Key phrases:

Auteurship is the anti-slop layer.

The Auteur should be the source of doctrine, not the bottleneck of action.

3. Doctrine

Doctrine deserves its own subsection between Auteurs and Agents.

Definition:

Doctrine is strategy made executable.

It should include:

* Current diagnosis.
* Guiding policy.
* Strategic tradeoffs.
* Anti-goals.
* Kill criteria.
* Decision rights.
* Examples of good judgment.
* Examples of tempting but wrong local optimization.
* Current known contradictions.
* Version history.
* Update owner.
* Escalation conditions.
* Agent-readable instructions and evals.

Key line:

Strategy that cannot be executed by agents is not yet doctrine.

4. Agents

Agents are the scalable execution layer.

They do not merely “do tasks.” They act from some premise-set. The question is whether that premise-set is canonical, fresh, and bounded.

Key distinction:

A generic agent produces plausible output. A doctrine-bound agent produces organizationally coherent output.

Agents need:

* Current doctrine.
* Retrieval from canonical sources.
* Permissions.
* Evals.
* Stop conditions.
* Escalation triggers.
* Blast-radius limits.
* Output review proportional to risk.
* Feedback paths into doctrine.

5. Actors

Actors are the boundary organs of the organization.

They interface with customers, partners, regulators, employees, markets, physical operations, ambiguous social meaning, trust, and accountability.

They are not just “humans doing what agents cannot.” Better:

Actors touch reality and bring back the signals doctrine cannot yet encode.

Actors preserve reality-contact.

Without Actors, Auteurs and Agents can become coherent but false.

Key line:

An Actorless organization is a hallucination with payroll.

6. The loop

The whole system should be drawn as a loop:

Auteur → Doctrine → Agents → Output → Actors → Reality-contact → Contradiction → Ritual Time → Revised Doctrine → Run Time

This is the post-AGI metabolism of the firm.

7. Failure modes

Include a failure-mode section.

Auteur Bottleneck
The Auteur remains the only person who can interpret doctrine, so action cannot scale.

Auteur Delusion
The top vision overwhelms reality-contact.

Agentic Slop
Agents produce endless plausible output without sufficient taste, context, or kill criteria.

Actorless Execution
Agents execute doctrine that no one is testing against the world.

Coherent Wrongness
The organization is beautifully aligned around a false premise.

Shadow Doctrine
Teams or agents act from local, stale, or political versions of strategy.

Doctrine Rot
The doctrine is technically available but no longer current.

Agency Inversion
Marginal agency has turned negative because coherence capacity is exhausted.

8. Success state

Name the successful form:

The Bounded Swarm

Definition:

A high-agency organization whose agents and humans can act autonomously because doctrine is fresh, permissions are bounded, reality-contact is alive, and Ritual Time can update the system when the map melts.

New Appendix F: Minimum Viable Coherence Program

Purpose

Give readers a practical way to run the new theory without needing a full reorganization.

Suggested structure

1. Publish a doctrine packet

Every team should have a short, current, versioned doctrine packet.

Minimum contents:

* Current diagnosis.
* What we are trying to do.
* What we are not trying to do.
* Strategic tradeoffs.
* Anti-goals.
* Kill criteria.
* Decision rights.
* Current contradictions.
* Examples of good judgment.
* Examples of tempting but wrong local optimization.
* Agent instructions and escalation rules.

2. Build a doctrine-to-agent interface

Every significant agentic workflow should answer:

* Which doctrine version is it using?
* Who owns that doctrine?
* What can it do without review?
* What must it escalate?
* What is its blast radius?
* How is it evaluated?
* How does feedback return?

3. Run a weekly context checksum

Ask humans and agents the same five questions:

1. What are we trying to do?
2. Why now?
3. What are we refusing?
4. What would change our mind?
5. What local win would be globally wrong?

Divergence is a signal of context debt.

4. Maintain a contradiction ledger

Track unresolved contradictions between:

* Strategy and incentives.
* Customer reality and product roadmap.
* Doctrine and agent behavior.
* Public commitments and operational capacity.
* Metrics and lived reality.
* Auteur intent and Actor feedback.

5. Install agency WIP limits

Cap active agentic initiatives the same way a factory caps WIP.

Do not allow every team to launch unlimited automations, experiments, campaigns, docs, and workflows simply because capacity is abundant.

Key line:

When coherence is the constraint, agency WIP is inventory before the bottleneck.

6. Add a permission ladder for agents

Suggested levels:

* Level 0: Suggest only.
* Level 1: Draft with human approval.
* Level 2: Execute reversible internal actions.
* Level 3: Execute reversible external actions with monitoring.
* Level 4: Execute irreversible or customer-visible actions only under explicit doctrine and escalation.
* Level 5: Autonomous high-stakes action, allowed only where doctrine, evals, and Actor feedback are mature.

7. Create decision memory

Every major decision should preserve:

* Decision made.
* Owner.
* Premises.
* Alternatives rejected.
* Tradeoffs accepted.
* What would change the decision.
* Doctrine affected.
* Agents/workflows affected.

8. Create a deletion cadence

Monthly or quarterly, kill:

* Stale prompts.
* Old automations.
* Zombie initiatives.
* Duplicated docs.
* Inactive dashboards.
* Obsolete policies.
* Misleading metrics.
* Abandoned agent workflows.

Key line:

When creation becomes cheap, deletion becomes sacred.

9. Preserve Actor feedback

Every doctrine-bound agentic system needs real-world contact.

Ask:

* Which Actors touch the world this workflow affects?
* How do they report contradiction?
* Can they stop or flag the workflow?
* Are their signals visible to Auteurs and doctrine stewards?

10. Ritualize doctrine update

When reality falsifies doctrine, do not merely patch the prompt.

Run Ritual Time.

The output should be:

* What died?
* What survived?
* What changed?
* What is now binding?
* Where does it live in Run Time?
* Which agents and workflows must be updated?

Important conceptual guardrails

1. Do not replace epimetabolism with coherence

The book should not say, “Actually, coherence is the bottleneck, not metabolism.”

Better:

Coherence is the Run Time scaling constraint. Epimetabolism is the Ritual Time capacity that restores and raises the coherence ceiling.

2. Do not make coherence sound like alignment theater

Coherence must not mean everyone agrees, obeys, or repeats the same words.

Better:

Coherence is regulated variety, not sameness.

3. Do not overstate the electrification analogy

The factory story is a powerful analogy, not a proof.

Use it this way:

Not because AGI is electricity in every respect, but because both are general-purpose technologies whose full value required redesigning the organization around the new bottleneck.

4. Do not make Auteurs autocrats

The Auteur provides vision, taste, and doctrine. The Auteur should not personally mediate every decision.

Guardrail:

The Auteur is the source of doctrine, not the bottleneck of action.

5. Do not make agents unaccountable Actors

Agents can execute. Actors bear reality-contact and accountability.

Guardrail:

Agents can act, but Actors must remain responsible for the boundary with the world.

6. Do not let Run Time absorb Ritual Time

This is the biggest internal risk.

Run Time coherence is not a substitute for Ritual Time. A perfectly coherent organization can still be wrong. That is why Ritual Time remains essential.

Guardrail:

Run Time preserves fidelity to the current map. Ritual Time changes the map.

The final integrated thesis

The revised Dionysus Program should be able to say:

The first AGI pressure is epistemic: knowledge melts faster. Ritual Time exists to metabolize that melt into new roles, norms, stories, commitments, and doctrine without burning Ren. The second AGI pressure is agentic: action scales faster. Run Time exists to maximize coherent agency under current doctrine without exceeding the organization’s coherence ceiling. The post-AGI organization therefore needs both: the Dionysian capacity to update the map, and the Apollonian discipline to let many humans and agents act from the map while it still holds.

That is the new synthesis.
