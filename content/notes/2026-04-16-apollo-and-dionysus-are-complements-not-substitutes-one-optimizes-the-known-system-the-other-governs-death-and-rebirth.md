The social cost of missing this pattern is brutal: the dashboard gets sharper while the room gets colder.

That’s the Apollo-only organization. It knows how to optimize the known system, but it has no safe way to let an obsolete one die.

You see it when uncertainty gets hidden until it shows up as waste, when postmortems turn polite, when people over-insure every plan to protect themselves. On a night paving job, the trucks stack up and idle in the dark. By then it’s too late. The waste was decided hours earlier, when over-ordering was the only socially admissible move.

What drives it is a category error. Apollo and Dionysus are not substitutes. Apollo governs run time: measure, predict, control. Dionysus governs death and rebirth: the public killing of bad explanations without sacrificing the people who held them.

Ignore that, and you become efficient at defending yesterday while tomorrow burns in the dark.
