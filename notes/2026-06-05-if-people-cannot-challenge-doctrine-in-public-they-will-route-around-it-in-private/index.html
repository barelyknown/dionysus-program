If people cannot challenge doctrine in public, they will route around it in private.

That is the Jester’s Privilege: the standing right of criticism made lawful inside the group. Not “speaking up” as a value, but a protected right to introduce a rival explanation without being treated as disloyal.

In the book’s terms, during Ritual Time, any participant may table a falsifiable alternative, and retaliation breaches the container. The heat goes onto the doctrine, assumption, or workflow, not the person. That is how criticism stays sharp without becoming scapegoating.

Why this matters now is simple: AI scales action faster than organizations update their maps. When doctrine goes stale, people compensate with private prompts, hidden spreadsheets, and shadow doctrine. Without protected speech, doctrinal drift does not stop. It goes underground. Build the Jester’s Privilege into the operating law, or you end up with many private maps under one logo.
